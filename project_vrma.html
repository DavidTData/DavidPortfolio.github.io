The Vancouver Rental Market

The most basic question I can ask is "how much does rent cost in Vancouver?"

One source that might have an answer is the Canadian Mortgage and Housing Corporation (CMHC). The CMHC conducts yearly surveys on rental markets across Canada, and usefully offers data on a neighborhood basis. Using data from their 2022 survey, I can use SQL to focus in on rental prices across Vancouver neighborhoods. For simplicity I will examine only one bedroom prices.

SQL Query:

SELECT survey_zone, one_bed 
	FROM combinedurms
	WHERE survey_year='2022' AND
	dwelling_type='Total' AND
	survey_zone IN ('Marpole',
			'East Hastings',
			'Southeast Vancouver',
			'South Granville & Oak',
			'Vancouver (CY)',
			'West End & Stanley Park',
			'Westside & Kerrisdale',
			'Kitsilano & Point Grey',
			'English Bay',
			'Downtown')
	ORDER BY one_bed
;

Across the entire city of Vancouver (listed as "Vancouver (CY)"), one-bedroom renters pay an average of $1629 per month. Perhaps unsurprisingly, residents of Downtown Vancouver pay the highest average rent for a one bedroom unit, at $1840 per month. As a general trend, one-bedroom renters tend to pay more the further north and west they live.

This table can easily be visualised as a bar chart, using Tableau.

The average for the whole of Vancouver is marked in red for clarity.

While this can answer the most basic question about Vancouver's rental market ("how much does rent cost?"), it suffers from a lack of context. For example, one might wonder how Vancouver rental prices compare to prices in other cities, or how rental prices in 2022 compare to prior years.

Helpfully, the CMHC rental survey includes every Canadian urban centre with over 10,000 residents, and has been conducted annually since 2006. I'll start with a cross-sectional analysis to get a sense of how Vancouver compares to other cities.

Naturally, comparing Vancouver to every urban centre with over 10,000 people is impractical - there are over 1000. Narrowing down areas presents a problem though. So far I have only analysised the City of Vancouver. But neighbouring cities are often grouped together into metropolitan areas - the City of Vancouver is just one of several municipalities in the Greater Vancouver metro area.

It is not uncommon for people to work in one city of a metro area and live in another, or to move between cities in the same metro area. As such, we would expect the rental markets of urban centres in the same metro area to be interconnected. This means that a comparison between the City of Vancouver and other cites could be more or less useful than a comparison between Greater Vancouver and other metro areas.

For completeness, I will make comparisons at both municipal and metro levels of analysis.

Within the Greater Vancouver Area, renters in the City of Vancouver pay the fifth highest one-bedroom prices, on average. This places them behind both the District of North Vancouver the City of North Vancouver, as well as the census subdivision "Greater Vancouver A" (which most prominently includes the University Endowment Lands) and West Vancouver.

Across the entire Greater Vancouver Area, a one-bedroom renter paid an average of $1543 a month in 2022.

Among major Canadian municipalities, the City of Vancouver ranks as the most expensive for one-bedroom renters. Vancouver's average of $1629 beat out more populace cities like Toronto ($1537), Calgary ($1224), and Montreal ($909)

Likewise, Greater Vancouver ranks as the most expensive major metro area in Canada in terms of one-bedroom rent. Once again, that places it ahead of the Greater Toronto Area ($1526) and Greater Montreal ($912), Canada's two most populated metro areas.

As we can see, the average monthly rent Vancouver-wide increased from $1067 in 2012 to $1629 in 2022. This represented an increase of $562, or 52.7%.

I should note that data from the CMHC's rental market surveys has a limitation, in that it represents the prices that current renters are paying. To illustrate the problem this poses, I will compare the CMHC's data with data from Zumper.

The CMHC estimates average rent by surveying current renters, which means it represents the average price among all current renters. However, we wouldn't expect all kinds of renters to pay similiar rents; in particular, we would expect long-time rents to pay less in rent that new renters, on average. This is a result of long-time renters being covered by rent control, among other factors. Because Zumper's data is taken from current rental listing, rather than current renters, the average provided by Zumper better reflects what someone looking to rent a new unit would expect to pay. On the other hand, the CMHC average better represents the rent paid by current renters.
